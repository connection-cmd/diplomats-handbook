How to deal with a difficult coworker

The passive-aggressive emailer, the credit-taker, the one who derails every meeting. You can't choose your colleagues — but you can choose how you carry yourself around them. Here's how to stay professional and protect your peace.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to keeping your cool at work

The trap with a difficult coworker is letting their behaviour set yours. The moment you match their heat or vent to the group, you've joined the problem. The stronger position is steady professionalism: calm, task-focused, and hard to provoke. That's what colleagues and managers remember.

Reframe before you react

A useful private thought: this isn't really about me — they're struggling with something and handing me the overflow. Difficult behaviour often comes from stress, pressure, or plain miscommunication rather than malice. Assuming the more charitable explanation keeps you calm and, surprisingly often, turns out to be true.

Keep it professional and boundaried

  • Stay task-focused. Keep exchanges about the work, not the personality. Short, clear, courteous.
  • Don't feed the drama. Decline the gossip, resist the venting, keep your side clean.
  • Name behaviour, not character. "When the deadline moved without telling me" — not "you're impossible."

Have the direct conversation

Often the thing that actually resolves it is a calm one-to-one: "I've noticed some friction between us lately, and I'd like to find a way to work together more smoothly — for the team's sake." It's respectful, hard to argue with, and shows a maturity that reflects far better on you than silent resentment ever would.

When to document and escalate

If the behaviour crosses a line — harassment, discrimination, sabotage — or a direct talk changes nothing, start keeping a simple record of incidents with dates. Then bring it to your manager as a problem you want help solving, not a grievance. "I've tried to resolve this directly and it's affecting my work — I'd value your advice" lands as professionalism, not complaint.

Common questions

How do you deal with a difficult coworker professionally?

Stay calm and task-focused, avoid gossip, and address the specific behaviour. A direct, low-heat conversation often resolves more than weeks of resentment.

What should you not do?

Don't vent to other coworkers, assume malice, or retaliate. All three hurt your reputation and escalate things. Keep your side professional.

When should you escalate to HR?

When it becomes harassment, discrimination, or sabotage, and after a direct talk fails. Document incidents and frame it as seeking help, not complaining.
