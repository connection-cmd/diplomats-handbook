How to give constructive criticism

The same feedback can spur someone to do their best work or quietly crush them — it's all in the delivery. Good criticism is specific, kind, aimed at the work, and always points forward. Here's how to give it well.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to feedback that builds

Criticism goes wrong when it's vague, personal, or dead-ended. "This isn't good enough" tells someone they've failed without telling them how to succeed. Real constructive feedback does the opposite: it names the specific issue, explains why it matters, and hands over a way to fix it.

Focus on the work, not the person

Describe what you saw, not who they are. "Three of the figures didn't match the summary" is fixable; "you're careless" is a wound. Keeping feedback on the work lets the person act on it without feeling attacked — and keeps their goodwill intact.

Observation, impact, path forward

The most useful feedback has three parts: what happened, its effect, and a suggestion. "The intro buries the main point (observation), so readers may miss it (impact) — what if we led with the result? (path forward)" That last part is what makes it constructive rather than just critical.

Deliver it with care

  • Privately. Never correct someone in front of others.
  • Specifically. One or two clear points beat a vague pile.
  • As a question, where you can. "What if we tried…?" invites collaboration, not defence.
  • With genuine warmth. Open on what's working, and mean it.

Go easy on the "sandwich"

The old trick of hiding criticism between two compliments can backfire — it muddies the message and makes praise feel like a setup. Better to lead with sincere positives, be clear and kind about what to improve, and close with encouragement and a concrete next step. Honesty wrapped in warmth, not disguised in it.

Common questions

How do you give constructive criticism?

Focus on the work, be specific about what and why, and offer a path forward. Deliver it privately, kindly, and aimed at making the work better.

What's the best way to phrase criticism?

Observation, impact, suggestion. Describe the work ("three figures didn't match"), not the person ("careless"). Framing a fix as a question invites collaboration.

Should you use the "feedback sandwich"?

Be careful — burying criticism between compliments can muddy the message. Better: open with genuine positives, be clear about the fix, end with encouragement.
