How to give your boss feedback (without it backfiring)

Telling the person who writes your review that something isn't working takes nerve — and technique. Done well, upward feedback marks you as thoughtful and trustworthy. Done badly, it lingers. Here's how to make it land.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to managing up with tact

Feedback to a manager is a matter of framing far more than courage. The same point can read as a complaint or as a contribution depending on how you set it up. The aim is to sound like someone helping the team succeed, not someone grading the boss.

Set the ground before you speak

  • Ask permission. "Would you be open to a bit of feedback on how the projects are being run?" A yes turns you from critic into invited advisor.
  • Do it privately. Never in a group. A one-to-one or a quiet call lets tone and body language carry the goodwill.
  • Pick the moment. Not in the heat of a mistake, and not in front of an audience. Calm beats cathartic.

The framing that works: observation, impact, request

Describe what you saw, the effect it had, and what would help — and keep judgement out of it. Say verbs about the situation, not adjectives about the person. Compare:

Instead of "You're really disorganised with the briefs," try "When the brief shifts midweek, we lose a day re-planning — could we lock the scope on Mondays?" The first attacks who they are; the second names a pattern and offers a fix.

Phrases you can borrow

  • On workload: "I can take this on, but only if we move the deadline on [X] — otherwise something slips."
  • On mixed signals: "Can we talk through [X]? I'm getting conflicting steers and want to get it right."
  • On after-hours pressure: "I really value how hard you work — I think some of the team feel they have to match your late nights. Could we set clearer expectations there?"
  • On delegation: "Handing more of [X] to us would stretch our skills and free you up for the bigger calls."

Read the room first

Not every manager has earned the same candour. If yours actively asks for input, be generous with it. If they've never invited it and bristle at challenge, start small — one low-stakes, easily-actioned point — and let a good result buy you the credibility for bigger conversations later.

Common questions

How do you give your boss feedback without offending them?

Ask first, keep it private, and describe what you observed rather than judging who they are. Lead with a shared goal and offer a concrete suggestion, not just a problem.

Is it appropriate to give your manager feedback?

Yes — when it's welcome, private, and about the work rather than their character. Read the relationship first and build trust before raising bigger things.

What's the best way to phrase criticism to your boss?

Observation plus impact plus a request: what happened, its effect, and what would help. Frame it as a question — "Could we try…?" — to invite collaboration.
