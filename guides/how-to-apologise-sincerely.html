The Apology

How to apologise properly (and mean it)

A real apology repairs; a defensive one deepens the wound. The difference is a handful of words — and the order you put them in.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to the conversations you'd rather not have

Most botched apologies fail in the same way: they are secretly about the apologiser. "I'm sorry if you felt that way." "I'm sorry, but you have to understand…". Each of these smuggles in a defence, and the other person hears it instantly. A sincere apology does the harder, simpler thing — it stays with their hurt long enough to name it.

The anatomy of a sincere apology

Four parts, in order. Skip one and it rings hollow.

  1. Name the act. Specific, not vague. "I'm sorry I raised my voice," not "I'm sorry things got heated."
  2. Own it without excuses. No "but." The moment a "but" arrives, the apology becomes a preamble to a defence.
  3. Acknowledge the effect. Show you understand what it cost them: "That must have been humiliating in front of the team."
  4. Offer to put it right. "I'll correct it with them tomorrow." Repair is what turns words into trust.
Put together

"I'm sorry I interrupted you in the meeting. There's no excuse — it was your point to make, and I took it. I imagine that felt dismissive. I'll flag with the team that the idea was yours."

The phrases that quietly ruin an apology

The conditional"I'm sorry if anyone was offended." — blames their reaction, not your act.
The buried defence"I'm sorry, but I was under a lot of pressure." — the "but" erases everything before it.
The vanishing subject"Mistakes were made." — no one did anything; nothing can be forgiven.

Don't argue the feeling — name the act

If someone is hurt, resist the urge to litigate whether they should be. You will never win it, and winning it isn't the point. Grant the feeling, name what you did, and let the repair do the rest.

Common questions

What makes an apology sincere?

It names the specific act, owns it without excuses, acknowledges the effect on the other person, and offers to put it right. The test: it centres their hurt, not your discomfort.

Why is "I'm sorry if you felt that way" a bad apology?

Because "if you felt that way" makes their reaction the problem rather than your action. Name the act instead: "I'm sorry I interrupted you."

How do I apologise at work without over-admitting fault?

Apologise for the specific impact, not a vague wrong: "I'm sorry the report was late and it held up your team." Then say what you'll change.
