The Apology

How to apologize to someone you hurt

A real apology isn't about clearing your conscience — it's about repairing the other person's trust. That means owning what you did, feeling the weight of it, and asking for nothing in return. Here's how to do it well.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to making it right

Most apologies fail because they're secretly about the person apologizing — their guilt, their need to move on. A good one keeps its focus entirely on the person you hurt. Get that right and even a serious rupture can heal; get it wrong and "sorry" makes things worse.

The anatomy of a real apology

  • Name it plainly. "I'm sorry I broke your confidence and told others what you shared." No fog, no passive voice.
  • Show you understand the harm. "I can see that made you feel exposed and let down." This is the part most apologies skip — and the part that heals.
  • Say what changes. "I won't repeat things you tell me in private again." A promise, not a plea.
  • Then stop. Hand it over and let them respond in their own time.

The phrases that ruin an apology

Three "apologies" aren't apologies at all. "I'm sorry if you were offended" blames their reaction. "I'm sorry, but…" deletes everything before the "but" with an excuse. "I'm sorry you feel that way" sidesteps your action entirely. If the word "if," "but," or "you feel" appears, start again.

When they're still hurt

Forgiveness is theirs to give on their schedule, not yours. Say your piece sincerely, then resist the urge to defend yourself or press for a resolution. "I understand you're still upset, and that's fair — I'm not asking you to be okay right now. I just needed you to know I'm truly sorry." Patience is itself part of the repair.

Common questions

What makes a genuine apology?

Naming what you did without excuses, showing you understand the hurt, and saying how you'll do better — then giving them room to respond.

What should you not say?

Avoid "I'm sorry if you were offended," "I'm sorry, but…," and "I'm sorry you feel that way." Own your action, not their feelings about it.

How do you apologize when they're still angry?

Say your piece sincerely, then let them be angry without defending yourself. Don't push for instant forgiveness — repair takes time.
