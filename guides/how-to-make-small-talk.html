How to make small talk with anyone

Small talk isn't small. It's the doorway to every friendship, deal, and connection you'll ever make. And it's a skill, not a gift — a handful of openers, good questions, and genuine attention will carry you through any room.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to easy conversation

Most people dread small talk because they think they need to be witty. They don't. The people who are wonderful to talk to are rarely the cleverest — they're the most interested. Your job isn't to perform; it's to make the other person feel easy and heard.

Open on what you share

The simplest opener draws on the one thing you already have in common: the moment you're both in. The event, the queue, the food, the weather. "Quite a turnout — how do you know the host?" works because it's low-stakes and hands them an easy answer. A warm introduction and their name repeated back does the rest.

Ask questions that open people up

  • "What's kept you busy lately?" — broader and warmer than "what do you do?"
  • "How did you get into that?" — invites a story, not a fact.
  • "What's your story?" — disarming, open, and oddly delightful to answer.
  • "That's interesting — tell me more." — the six words that keep any conversation alive.

Climb the ladder

Think of topics as a pyramid. The base is obligatory ("how was your weekend?"); the common mistake is camping there. Once you've broken the ice, move a rung up — toward what lights them up, a shared interest, a real opinion. That ascent is what turns polite chatter into an actual connection.

Be present, and know when to leave

Put the phone away; nothing says "you matter" more clearly. Notice the details — the book in their hand, the logo on their shirt — and follow them. And when it's time, exit warmly: "This has been great — I'm going to grab a drink and mingle a little." A good goodbye is part of good small talk.

Common questions

How do you start small talk with a stranger?

Open on something you both share — the venue, the queue — then add a light question: "Great turnout — how do you know the host?" The shared moment lowers the stakes.

What questions keep a conversation going?

Open-ended ones: "What's kept you busy lately?", "How did you get into that?" Then "tell me more." Curiosity does the work.

What should you avoid?

Politics and religion with new acquaintances, leading with "what do you do?", and your phone. Being present matters most.
