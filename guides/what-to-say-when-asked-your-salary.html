The Question

What to say when someone asks how much you make

The table goes quiet, and every option feels wrong — lie, overshare, or bristle. There's a fourth way: answer the moment, not the number. Here's how to stay warm, keep your privacy, and move the conversation on.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to tact under pressure

A salary question isn't really a request for a figure. It's a small test of poise. Handle it well and you look composed and self-assured; handle it badly and you either give away more than you meant to or turn a light moment sharp. The goal is to close the topic without punishing the person for asking.

The three good moves

  • Deflect with warmth. "Enough to keep me in good coffee and bad opinions — what about you, what do you do?" A little humour and a question back, and the spotlight moves on.
  • Give a range, not a number. If it's a peer with a real reason to know: "For this kind of role it tends to run somewhere in the X to Y band." Useful, without exposing your exact pay.
  • Set a clean boundary. "I'd rather not get into numbers, if that's alright." Said lightly, with a smile, it's not cold — it's simply the end of that subject.

What quietly backfires

Two responses land worse than the question ever could. The first is "That's a rude thing to ask" — it may be true, but it punishes the whole room for one person's slip and makes you look prickly. The second is the over-explained non-answer — a long, awkward speech about why you don't discuss money, which draws more attention to the number than a short line ever would. Brevity is your friend.

Match your answer to who's asking

A nosy acquaintance at a party wants a moment of drama or comparison — give them warmth and a subject change. A trusted colleague in your exact role may be trying to work out whether they're underpaid — there, a shared range can be a genuine kindness and a source of leverage for you both. Read the motive, then choose the move.

Common questions

Is it rude to ask someone how much they make?

In most social settings, yes — a direct salary question to someone you don't know well is intrusive, and you're free to deflect. The exception is a trusted peer in the same role, where sharing can help you both.

How do I politely not answer?

Keep it light and hand it back: "Enough to keep me out of trouble — what about you?" Or be plain: "I'd rather not get into numbers, if that's alright." Both close the topic cleanly.

Should I tell a coworker my salary?

Your call — and often legally protected. Sharing with a trusted peer can expose unfair gaps and help a raise case; sharing widely tends to cause friction. Ask who benefits before you answer.
