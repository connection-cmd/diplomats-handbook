What to say to someone who is grieving

The fear of saying the wrong thing keeps many of us from saying anything — and the silence hurts more. You don't need perfect words. You need to be sincere, be present, and keep showing up.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to comfort in hard moments

Grief doesn't ask you to fix it. It asks you to witness it. The most comforting thing you can offer isn't wisdom or a silver lining — it's the plain acknowledgement that someone they loved is gone, and that you are here. Short and heartfelt beats long and careful.

Simple things that help

  • "I'm so sorry. I'm here." Plain, warm, and enough. It acknowledges the loss and your presence in one breath.
  • Name the person. "I keep thinking about [name]." Hearing their name spoken is often a quiet relief, not a fresh wound.
  • Share a memory. "I'll never forget how [they did X]." It tells the grieving person their loved one mattered to others too.
  • "There's nothing right to say — but I'm not going anywhere." Honesty about the limits of words is itself a comfort.

What to avoid

Steer clear of anything that explains or minimises the loss: "everything happens for a reason," "at least they had a long life," "they're in a better place," "time heals." However kindly meant, these ask the grieving person to feel better on your schedule. Anything that begins with "at least" is worth stopping before it starts.

Do something, not just say something

"Let me know if you need anything" puts the work on them. Offer something concrete instead — drop off a meal, handle an errand, take the kids for an afternoon, or simply sit in the quiet with them. And remember the second act of grief: keep checking in weeks and months on, when the world has moved along and they haven't.

Common questions

What's the best thing to say?

Something short and sincere: "I'm so sorry. I'm here, and I'm not going anywhere." Acknowledge the loss and make clear you're present.

What should you not say?

Avoid phrases that minimise or explain — "everything happens for a reason," "at least…," "they're in a better place." Let them feel what they feel.

What can I do besides saying something?

Offer something specific — a meal, an errand, your company — and keep checking in weeks and months later. Steady presence comforts most.
