The Difficult Moment

How to tell someone they hurt you

Say nothing and the resentment festers; say it badly and it becomes a row. There's a middle path: calm, specific, and aimed at repair. Here's how to be heard instead of fought.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to hard, honest conversations

Telling someone they hurt you is an act of trust, not attack. The goal isn't to win or to punish — it's to be understood and to mend the relationship. Everything below serves that: keeping the other person able to hear you, rather than bracing against you.

Cool down, then prepare

Don't raise it in the heat of the moment. Step back, and work out what actually hurt and why. Walking in with a clear, specific point — rather than a flood of grievance — is the difference between a conversation and a fight.

Lead with "I", not "you"

This is the whole game. "You never include me" is an accusation, and the other person will argue the "never." "I felt left out when the plans were made without me" is your experience, and it's almost impossible to dispute. Name the specific action and the feeling it caused, and keep character out of it.

Don't assume their intent

Resist the urge to explain why they did it. Instead, get curious: "Can you help me understand what was going on for you?" Most hurt comes from carelessness or a misread, not cruelty. Asking rather than accusing lets them explain without shame — and often the whole thing softens right there.

Aim for repair, not just release

Venting relieves you; repair mends the bond. Say what would help going forward — "I'd just love a heads-up next time" — and leave room for them to respond and make it right. And if the conversation heats up, it's fine to pause: "Can we take a breather and come back to this?" Calm, resumed, beats heated and finished.

Common questions

How do you tell someone they hurt you without a fight?

Cool down, then use "I" statements about a specific moment: "When the plan changed without telling me, I felt sidelined." Ask their side, and aim for repair.

Why do people get defensive?

Blame triggers self-defence. "You always ignore me" starts an argument; "I felt ignored when…" keeps the focus on your experience, which is harder to argue with.

Should you assume they meant to hurt you?

No. Instead of telling them why they did it, ask: "Can you help me understand what was going on for you?" Most hurt is carelessness, not malice.
