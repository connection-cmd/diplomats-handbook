The Apology

How to respond to an apology

"Sorry" lands, and suddenly the pressure's on you. Do you forgive? Say it's fine when it wasn't? You have more options than that — ways to receive an apology with grace and honesty, whether or not you're ready to let it go.

From The Diplomat's Handbook · a guide to graceful receiving

Receiving an apology well is its own skill. The reflex "it's okay" often isn't true, and it can quietly excuse what happened while burying how you feel. The kinder, more honest move is to acknowledge the apology for what it is — and to be truthful about where you are.

If you're ready to move on

Accept it warmly and let it land: "Thank you for saying that — it means a lot, and we're good." You don't have to pretend nothing happened; simply naming that you appreciate the apology closes the loop with warmth on both sides.

If you're not ready yet

Honesty beats a forced "it's fine." "I really appreciate you apologising. I'm still a little hurt and I need some time — but this helps." You can recognise the apology as sincere while being clear that feelings and trust mend on their own schedule. This protects the relationship far better than a resentful pretence.

Skip the automatic "it's okay"

If it truly was minor, "it's okay" is fine. But when it wasn't, that phrase does quiet damage — it tells the other person the behaviour was acceptable, and it teaches you to swallow your feelings. "Thank you for apologising" accepts the gesture without rewriting what happened.

When the apology falls short

Sometimes a "sorry" comes with a "but," or dodges the real thing. You can gently name it: "I appreciate that — the part that mattered to me was…" It gives them a chance to hear what actually needs acknowledging, and turns a hollow apology into a real repair.

Common questions

What do you say when someone apologizes?

If you're ready to move on, accept warmly: "Thank you for saying that — I appreciate it." You don't have to say "it's fine" if it wasn't.

What if you're not ready to forgive?

Be honest without slamming the door: "I appreciate the apology. I'm still hurt and need some time — but this helps." Feelings mend on their own schedule.

Should you always say "it's okay"?

No — it can excuse the behaviour and bury your feelings. If it was minor, fine; if not, "thank you for apologising" is better.
